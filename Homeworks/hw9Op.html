Encryption Methods and Statistical Properties

Substitution Ciphers

Historical Context: Dating back to Julius Caesar's time, substitution ciphers were the primary encryption method until the 20th century.

Mathematical Foundation:

  • Bijective mapping function f: P → C where P is plaintext alphabet and C is ciphertext alphabet
  • Key space size: 26! (approximately 4.03 × 10^26) possible combinations

Statistical Properties:

  • Preserves individual letter frequencies: f(a) occurs as often as a
  • Maintains relative frequency distributions: P(f(a))/P(f(b)) = P(a)/P(b)
  • Vulnerable to frequency analysis attacks due to language patterns
  • Zero entropy increase: H(plaintext) = H(ciphertext)

Cryptanalysis Methods:

  • Frequency Analysis: Using known language statistics
  • Pattern Recognition: Identifying repeated sequences
  • Word Structure Analysis: Common words like "the", "and"

Permutation Ciphers

Mathematical Foundation:

  • Bijective function π: {1,...,n} → {1,...,n} where n is block length
  • Key space size: n! possible permutations

Statistical Properties:

  • Preserves global frequency distribution
  • Disrupts local statistics: bigrams, trigrams
  • Position-dependent entropy: H(pos_i) remains constant
  • Block-size dependent security: larger blocks = more security

Attack Vectors:

  • Known-plaintext attacks using pattern matching
  • Statistical analysis of block boundaries
  • Computational complexity: O(n!) for brute force

Modern Encryption (AES/RSA)

AES Properties:

  • Block cipher with 128/192/256-bit keys
  • Confusion through SubBytes transformation
  • Diffusion through MixColumns and ShiftRows
  • Statistical properties:
    • Uniform output distribution: P(c_i) ≈ 1/256 for all bytes
    • High avalanche effect: changing 1 bit affects ~50% of output bits
    • Maximum entropy: H(ciphertext) ≈ 8 bits per byte

RSA Properties:

  • Based on modular exponentiation: c = m^e mod n
  • Security depends on factoring large numbers
  • Statistical independence between input and output
  • Probabilistic encryption recommended for semantic security

Frequency Analysis & Statistical Tests

English Language Statistics:

  • Single letter frequencies:
    • E (12.7%), T (9.1%), A (8.2%), O (7.5%), I (7.0%)
    • Entropy: ~4.2 bits per character
  • N-gram Analysis:
    • Digrams: TH (3.56%), HE (3.07%), IN (2.43%)
    • Trigrams: THE (3.51%), AND (1.59%), ING (1.15%)

Statistical Tests for Encryption:

  • Entropy Tests:
    • Shannon Entropy: H = -Σ p_i log₂(p_i)
    • Min-entropy: H_∞ = -log₂(max(p_i))
  • Randomness Tests:
    • Chi-square test for uniformity
    • Serial correlation coefficient
    • Runs test for independence

Statistical Analysis: Frequency Distribution and Entropy

Frequency Distribution Changes

The substitution cipher in this exercise preserves the overall frequency distribution of the plaintext message. For instance, if a letter like 'L' appears three times in the plaintext, its substitute, e.g., 'N,' will also appear three times in the ciphertext. However, after the permutation step (reversing the order of the encrypted text), local patterns such as digraphs or trigraphs (e.g., "LL" becoming "NN") are disrupted. This disruption can obscure common patterns and make frequency-based cryptanalysis more difficult.

While the global frequency distribution remains the same (important for language patterns like 'E' being the most common letter in English), permutation disrupts localized statistical predictability, making it harder for attackers to guess specific sequences.

Significance in Cryptography:

  • Substitution Cipher: The cipher alone is vulnerable to frequency analysis attacks, as it does not obscure the letter frequency.
  • Permutation Step: Adds an additional layer of security by fragmenting recognizable patterns, improving the cipher's resistance to attacks such as digraph and trigram analysis.

Entropy Considerations

Entropy measures the uncertainty or randomness in a message:

  • Original Message Entropy: English text typically has an entropy of about 3.5 bits per character due to its inherent structure and predictability.
  • Post-Substitution Entropy: Theoretical entropy of the cipher text increases slightly (~4.7 bits/character) because of uniform substitution probabilities, but the actual entropy remains limited by the redundancy in English text.
  • Post-Permutation Entropy: The reversal of letters does not increase the true randomness but disrupts recognizable sequences. This does not significantly increase the entropy but makes cryptanalysis harder.

Security and Unpredictability: Higher entropy in the encrypted message indicates greater security, as it suggests that the message is less predictable and more resistant to attacks. However, the substitution-permutation combination in this exercise falls short of achieving the entropy levels seen in advanced cryptographic systems like AES (~8 bits per character).

Contrast with RSA Concepts

RSA encryption operates differently:

  • Frequency Distribution: RSA encryption does not rely on substitution but instead applies modular arithmetic (e.g., c = m^e mod n). This process randomizes the ciphertext without preserving the plaintext's frequency distribution.
  • Key Management: Unlike the substitution cipher, RSA uses key pairs (public and private keys) to ensure security. Frequency analysis is irrelevant because the ciphertext's statistical structure is entirely unrelated to the plaintext.
  • Security: RSA achieves security through computational difficulty (factoring large primes), while this toy cipher relies on obscurity and pattern disruption.

Final Thoughts on Entropy and Security

Entropy is a cornerstone of cryptographic strength. High entropy implies unpredictability, reducing the likelihood of successful attacks based on patterns or frequency. Systems like AES excel by employing:

  • Complex Substitution: Highly nonlinear mappings through S-boxes.
  • Extensive Permutation and Mixing: Techniques like ShiftRows and MixColumns disrupt patterns.
  • Key-Dependent XORs: Enhance randomness and security further.

Although the exercise omits the XOR steps for simplicity, they are critical in modern ciphers like AES for preventing predictable transformations and ensuring high entropy. The toy cipher demonstrates foundational concepts but highlights the limitations of simple substitution and permutation when compared to robust cryptographic algorithms.
