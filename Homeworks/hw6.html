Fundamental Theorem of Calculus

The FTC has two main parts:

  1. If \( F(x) \) is defined as the integral of a function \( f(t) \) from a fixed point \( a \) to a variable point \( x \), then the derivative of \( F(x) \) is simply \( f(x) \). Mathematically:

    $$ F(x) = \int_{a}^{x} f(t) \, dt \Rightarrow F'(x) = f(x) $$

    Here, \( F(x) \) is the antiderivative of \( f(x) \), meaning \( F(x) \) is a function whose derivative is \( f(x) \). The idea is that if you integrate \( f(x) \), then take its derivative, you return to \( f(x) \).

  2. If \( f(x) \) is continuous on the interval \( [ a ,b ] \), then the integral of \( f(x) \) over \( [ a ,b ] \) can be computed using any antiderivative \( F(x) \) of \( f(x) \):

    $$ \int_{a}^{b} f(x) \, dx = F(b) - F(a) $$

    This part of the theorem allows us to compute the exact area under \( f(x) \) from \( x = a \) to \( x = b \) by evaluating an antiderivative at the endpoints.

Relationship with Density Functions and Cumulative Distribution Functions

For a continuous random variable \( X \), we define:

  1. Probability Density Function (PDF) \( f(x) \)

    2. This function describes the density of probability around any given point \( x \). For example, the height of a person can be modeled with a density function. The actual value of \( f(x) \) doesnâ€™t give probability directly but indicates how "concentrated" the probability is around \( x \).

  2. Cumulative Distribution Function (CDF) \( F(x) \)

    3. This function gives the cumulative probability up to point \( x \), defined as:

    $$ F(x) = P(X \leq x) = \int_{-\infty}^{x} f(t) \, dt. $$

    Here, \( F(x) \) represents the probability that \( X \leq x \).

Now, applying the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus to \( F(x) = \int_{-\infty}^{x} f(t) \, dt \):

  1. Differentiating the CDF Yields the PDF

    2. According to the FTC, since \( F(x) \) is the integral of \( f(t) \) from \( -\infty \) to \( x \), the derivative of \( F(x) \) with respect to \( x \) is:

    $$ F'(x) = f(x). $$

    This equation states that the rate of change of the cumulative probability (CDF) at any point \( x \) is exactly the probability density (PDF) at that point.

  2. Evaluating Probability over Intervals

    3. The FTC also tells us that the probability of \( X \) falling between two points \( a \) and \( b \) can be found by evaluating the CDF at these points:

    $$ P(a \leq X \leq b) = F(b) - F(a) = \int_{a}^{b} f(x) \, dx. $$

    Here, the FTC guarantees that the integral of the density function \( f(x) \) over \( [a, b] \) gives the increase in cumulative probability \( F(x) \) over this interval.
